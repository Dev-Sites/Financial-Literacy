Keeping records

Monitoring the profitability of a business begins with good record keeping. A basic record keeping system could include the following steps.

1. Recording transactions as they occur on source documents, for example, invoices, credit notes, purchase orders, receipts, cash register tapes, cheque butts, bank deposit slips.

2. Keeping a summary of transactions, for example, a day book to record sales and purchases

3. Organising and storing all records safely and securely.

Keeping well organised documents will help you analyse the business performance as quickly and efficiently as possible.

Remember also that some records may be required by the Taxation Department including:

  • sales records - tax invoices, receipts, cash register tapes, credit card statements, bank deposit books, bank account statements, cheque butts
  • purchase/expense records - invoices, purchase statements, cheque butts, bank account statements, credit card statements, records showing calculations of private use components of assets
  • year-end income tax records - motor vehicle expenses, debtors and creditors lists, stocktake sheets, depreciation schedules, capital gains tax records
  • records relating to payments to employees - tax file number declarations and withholding declarations, witholding variation notices, worker payment records, PAYG payment summaries, annual reports, superannuation records, records of any fringe benefits provided
  • PAYG withholding records relating to business payments - records of amounts withheld from payments where no ABN was quoted, a copy of any PAYG withholding voluntary agreements, records of voluntary agreement payments, PAYG payment summaries.
 

The actual records you need will depend on your particular business. An accountant, solicitor or tax professional can provide assistance.
