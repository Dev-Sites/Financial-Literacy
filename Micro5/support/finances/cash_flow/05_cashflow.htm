Cash flow budget

Here's a step-by-step summary to help prepare a cash flow budget.

Step one - time period

Decide on the period of time that the cash flow budget will cover, for example, a month, quarter, half-year or year.

Step two - estimate cash inflows (receipts)

For the time period, show each cash receipt you expect to receive. This could include money from a customer, a loan received, money from an asset sold and money you will contribute.

Step three - estimate cash outflows (payments)

For the time period, show each cash payment you expect to pay out. This could include a payment to a supplier, loan repayment, purchase of an asset, and money (drawings) you take for your own personal use.

Remember infrequent payments such as car registration and insurances. Also remember any tax payments. These payments could include GST collected from your customers, PAYG instalments of income tax, amounts you've withheld from employees' wages, and fringe benefits tax.

Step four - calculate cash balance

Add the estimated cash inflows to the amount of cash on hand at the beginning of the time period. Subtract the estimated cash outflows. The difference is the estimated ending cash balance. The cash balance becomes the cash on hand at the start of the next period.

Analysing the cash flow budget

The cash flow budget can show you if there are times when the business will be short of cash or have excess cash available.

This can help you make plans in advance to avoid cash shortages or to cope with the times when there are cash shortages.

Points to remember

If the business will be your main source of income, then the amount of available cash generated by the business must be sufficient to meet your personal expenses as well. The amount you will need to take from the business in order to cover your personal expenses is called drawings.

Contact a financial adviser or accountant for specific advice about taxation issues, cash flow and other questions regarding financing a micro business.
