Ongoing expenses

All businesses have ongoing expenses.

Sales related expenses are directly incurred in producing a product or providing a service. These expenses tend to vary according to the level of business activity.

Operating expenses support the functions of the business, but are not directly related to the manufacturing of a product or provision of a service. Some of these expenses vary and others are relatively fixed.

Sales related expenses

There is a clear relationship between the level of these expenses and the level of business activity. Some examples include:

  • direct materials - purchase of raw materials or finished product
  • packaging
  • direct labour - cost of workers directly involved in making the product/providing the service.
 

Practical example

If a potter uses 500 g of clay to produce one bowl, then 500 kg would be needed to produce 1,000 bowls. There is a direct relationship between the cost of the potter's raw materials (clay) and the level of activity, that is the number of bowls produced.

Operating expenses

Some operating expenses remain relatively constant and others vary.

Examples of expenses that tend to remain constant are:

  • rates
  • insurance premiums
  • vehicle registration
  • lease of plant and equipment
  • rent
 

Practical example

Joe operates his hairdressing business from a leased business premises. At the moment he does not utilise all the space. The cost of the lease doesn't change.

Examples of expenses that vary include:

  • fuel costs
  • motor vehicle maintenance
  • advertising
  • photocopying
  • telephone.
 

Practical example

Joe places advertisements for his hairdressing business in the local newspaper from time to time. There is no direct relationship between the business activity he performs, that is hairdressing and the amount of money paid for advertising.

A question to ponder

Can a sales related expense for one business be an operating expense for another?

Working capital and ongoing expenses

As a rough guide, it is suggested that you have sufficient working capital at start-up to cover all the ongoing expenses you will have to pay in the first four months. However, this amount will vary from industry to industry.
