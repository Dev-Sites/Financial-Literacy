Financial terms

If you have difficulty working out the difference between assets and income, or liabilities and expenses, here's a simple explanation.

Asset or income?

An asset is an item of value that a person owns, such as a home, car, bank account, etc. Income generally means money received.

If you sell an asset you receive income from the sale. For example, if you sell a car you own and receive $10,000, you have sold an asset and received income of $10,000. Of course, you'd also need to take into account how much you paid for the car in the first place to work out whether you made a profit or loss on the sale.

Liability or expense?

Liabilities and expenses both mean that you will be paying money out.

A liability is a debt that a person is legally required to repay at some time in the future, for example, a home mortgage or a car loan. An expense generally describes an amount spent in day-to-day living or a regular repayment required to pay off a liability.

Grocery bills and telephone accounts are expenses. A home loan repayment is also an expense, although the entire home loan is a liability.
